USP brainstorming
Wed, Mar 11, 2026

Summary:

The meeting reviewed and refined the company’s unique selling proposition (USP) and how to position the product versus competitors in the industrial software and systems market. The team clarified what a USP should convey, listed candidate strengths (functionality, security, reliability, flexibility, implementation and maintenance experience), and surveyed competitors including Siemens, Datastore, and Ignition. Feature work under development was reviewed—templating, responsive PC/tablet/phone design, multitenant microservices channelization, and real-time data and analytics—along with localization, timezone handling, and examples where long-term performance outperformed initial rivals.

Discussion then moved to organizing inputs: participants favored an affinity-style grouping limited and pragmatic in scope, preferring three groups but allowing up to five depending on audience needs (clients, technical staff, operators/managers). Integration constraints, speed, cost, and scalability were highlighted as priority evaluation parameters. Parallel work on the website will slow execution briefly to improve positioning before incremental rollout. Technical topics covered scripts and edge computing across devices, questions about local versus distributed execution, pricing and competitor defense concerns, a no-code application platform and its governance, mobile parity and UI customization, and integration readiness including an “NQTT” protocol and prepared connectors. Device management, batch configuration, ML integration, distributed systems complexity, security, and remote-control features were noted as important. Configuration gaps remain for mail and SMS senders, and a final agenda theme was not completed before closing.


Chapters & Topics:

Opening and logistics
The meeting opened with general check-ins, connectivity and arrival remarks from participants in the conference room. Participants exchanged brief logistical notes and polite acknowledgments before moving to agenda topics.

Purpose and definition of USP
The team asked why a clear USP is needed and discussed that a USP applies across services and products listed on the market. Participants emphasized the need to explain succinctly why customers should choose the company over competitors in short pitches.
* The team listed core strengths to include in the USP: functionality, logistical experience, security, reliability, flexibility, implementation and maintenance.
* The meeting aimed to define a clear USP for the product to distinguish it from competitors.
* The market includes established competitors such as Siemens, Datastore, Humulosity, Wunderware, Ignition, and the company must position itself against them.

Candidate strengths and foundational categories
The group identified six foundational categories to underpin the USP effort and listed core strengths including functionality, logistical experience, security, reliability, flexibility, and implementation/maintenance. They noted additional protocol points exist outside those six categories.
* The team proposed six foundational categories to structure the USP effort.

Product features and implementation examples
The discussion covered specific product features such as Boomerang-related functionality, templating to speed development, responsive design across devices, and automated reporting features. The team described signal/react behavior and implementation tracking as part of the product experience.
* Product features discussed for differentiation included templating for faster development, responsive design for PC/tablet/phone, and real-time analytics and monitoring.

Case reviews, multitenancy and real-time capabilities
Participants examined past competitive cases (InSpatial, bids lost but strong long-term performance) and discussed multitenant microservices channelization that achieved high view counts. The team highlighted real-time data analysis and monitoring as a capability to leverage for differentiation.
* A past bid was reviewed as an example where longer-term performance delivered advantage despite losing the initial competition.

Localization, access model and competitor analysis
The conversation moved to implementation constraints such as production vs. implementation permissions, open access and timezone/localization handling, and compared Siemens’ USP components like integration and industry-specific solutions. The group offered to discuss competitor USPs further but noted time constraints for deeper company-specific proposals.
* Localization and timezone handling were raised as technical considerations to include in the platform design.

Early meeting prompts and topic shift toward affinity grouping
The discussion opens with brief interjections and transitions before the topic of affinity grouping is raised. Participants ask whether attendees recall what "Affinity" means and confirm it was previously explained, setting up the decision to apply that method.
* The team will use an affinity-style method to group collected inputs into themes and build a matrix to identify overlaps and gaps.

Defining the grouping method and process
The team outlined a process to extract inputs, collect papers, define groups, and glue related items into a matrix to determine what is the same, unclear, irrelevant, or useful. Work should focus on five or six key points to identify viable groupings.

Group categories and scope decisions
Participants debated the nature of groups (technical, learning, business) and expressed uncertainty about exact categories. There was repeated emphasis on finding a pragmatic grouping approach rather than over-specifying categories.

Planning pace, website work, and constraints
The team agreed to proceed cautiously—slowing down execution while improving the website and then incrementally pushing progress. They reiterated uncertainties but affirmed ongoing work and a solid team presence.
* The team reports being solid and intends to proceed deliberately (slow down, then push).
* The website will be reviewed and used as a platform to build and iterate the work.

Group count, audience segmentation, and prioritization parameters
Integration complexity was raised as a constraint and the team recommended limiting groups (three preferred, up to five maximum). The priority decision should account for audience type, and speed, cost, expandability, and trustworthiness of results were highlighted as key parameters.
* Integration with other systems is complex and needs to be treated as a significant constraint.
* The group prefers to limit grouping to three clusters but will consider up to five if needed.
* Group definitions should reflect target audiences: potential clients, technical people, and smaller operators/managers.
* Speed and cost are the top parameters for decision-making and prioritization.

Short break planning, grouping mechanics, and next steps discussion
Participants planned to discuss details further after a break to finalize whether three groups suffice and to allow people to judge similarity across lists; they also noted potential operational issues and follow-ups with external parties.

UX, Neo4j security, start/stop challenges, and demo references
The conversation moved to UX practicality and Neo4j role-based security, with participants admitting repeated challenges in deciding where to start and stop work. A brief demonstration or screen share was referenced to show the current method and appearance.

The group noted uncertainty about local execution models and how voice/services may activate together.
